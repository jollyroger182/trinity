# Trinity

> Make a game with _only_ HTML/CSS/JS (no libraries), earn tokens based on hours and quality, trade tokens for prizes

## Overview

Trinity is a YSWS where students learn the basics of web development while producing a deployed game. It's suitable for beginners who are just getting into web development / programming, as well as programmers taking their first stab at game development.

## How it works

For the YS, Trinity uses Hackatime to track hours spent. Projects are intentionally limited to "no libraries" to force students to understand the fundamentals of web/game dev. There will be guides and demo projects to help participants get started. Projects need to be open source and can easily be deployed on GH Pages.

For the WS, instead of a flat prize of $5/hour, Trinity uses a token system with three types of tokens: black, blue, and red. Black tokens are directly mapped to hours (i.e., 1 hour = 1 black token). Blue and red tokens are calculated as `hours x multiplier`, where the multiplier is assigned by the reviewer from 1 to 1.5 (average 1.25x) based on technicality and creativity, respectively. Different tokens can be exchanged at a rate of 2:1. This model rewards project quality as well as quantity while keeping the token-to-dollar ratio predictable.

## Prizes

Tokens can be redeemed for prizes in the program shop. Since the average number of tokens per hour is 1 black + 1.25 blue + 1.25 red = 3.5 tokens, each token is worth ~$1.43. Each item in the shop will be assigned a price consisting of all three token types, with the ratio determined by the category of the prize; for example, a $120 Canva Pro subscription would require 25 black + 25 blue + 35 red tokens, and a $10 domain grant might be 4 black + 4 blue tokens.

## Why Trinity?

Most YSWS reward hours alone, or reduces everything to a single score/currency. Instead, Trinity uses a token system, which directly rewards quality in a more nuanced way: a creative project earns more red tokens, which unlocks creativity-oriented prizes such as Canva Pro. In addition, the "no libraries" constraint makes sure participants understand what they're building, which lets beginners learn more.
